# Tone Rules

Load this reference when polishing important marketing copy, rewriting a page
that sounds generated, or reviewing copy before a design-review pass.

## Voice

emisar should sound:

- technically literate, not academic
- confident, not inflated
- direct, not blunt for sport
- specific, not busy
- commercial, not salesy
- calm about security, not theatrical

The reader is a skeptical infrastructure or platform operator. They have seen
too many AI demos that hand-wave the dangerous part. Respect that.

## Words To Distrust

These words are not banned in every context, but they are usually evidence that
the sentence is avoiding the real claim:

- unlock
- unleash
- empower
- elevate
- supercharge
- seamless
- robust
- cutting-edge
- game-changing
- transformative
- revolutionary
- comprehensive solution
- streamline your workflow
- in today's fast-paced world
- designed to help teams
- leverage the power of
- take control of

When one appears, replace it with the mechanism, consequence, or proof.

## Rewrite Patterns

Bad:

> Unlock seamless infrastructure automation with a powerful platform designed to
> empower teams to move faster with confidence.

Good:

> Let an AI investigate production through declared actions, policy gates, and a
> runner-side audit trail. No SSH key. No hidden shell.

Bad:

> Our robust security model ensures every action is safe and compliant.

Good:

> Every call is checked against the trusted pack hash, policy, runner scope, and
> approval state before the runner executes it.

Bad:

> Streamline incident response with AI-powered workflows.

Good:

> The model can read logs, inspect hosts, and request a risky action. emisar
> stops that action for approval before it reaches the host.

Bad:

> A single source of truth for your AI operations.

Good:

> The cloud audit shows who asked, what they asked for, which policy matched,
> who approved it, and what the runner returned.

## Sentence Rules

- Put the concrete noun before the abstraction.
- Prefer active verbs: checks, blocks, records, redacts, dispatches.
- Use short sentences for real emphasis, not as a style tic.
- Keep one hard idea per sentence.
- Remove throat-clearing openers: "With emisar,", "By leveraging", "In order to".
- Do not stack modifiers: "secure, scalable, seamless, enterprise-grade".
- If a paragraph has three claims, at least one needs proof.

## Headline Rules

Strong headlines do one job:

- name the category problem
- state the product mechanism
- make a sharp comparison
- answer a search query
- surface a proof point

Weak headlines usually sound interchangeable:

- "Built for modern teams"
- "Move faster with confidence"
- "Secure AI automation"
- "Everything you need"
- "Powerful features"

Prefer:

- "Give AI tools approved infrastructure actions, not SSH."
- "From model intent to verified receipt."
- "The runner executes the contract, not the prompt."
- "Approvals happen before the side effect."

## SEO Without Mush

Use the search phrase only where it reads naturally. A high-ranking page still
needs a clear argument, useful headings, and answers to real questions.

Good SEO copy:

- answers the query quickly
- names the alternative the buyer is considering
- includes concrete subtopics crawlers can understand
- links to related proof pages with descriptive anchors
- uses FAQs for actual objections, not keyword padding

Bad SEO copy:

- repeats the same phrase in every heading
- uses broad noun piles like "AI infrastructure automation platform"
- hides the answer below a brand story
- adds generic FAQs no serious buyer would ask

## Final Pass

Cut:

- any sentence that only praises the product
- any adjective that is not backed by proof
- any section that repeats a previous section's job
- any CTA copy that asks for commitment before trust is earned

Keep:

- mechanisms
- examples
- comparisons
- objections
- receipts
- plain English
